Sima Berman Birth Family Investigation

This site documents our family investigation into the first family of Sima Berman, her siblings, her father, and the relatives whose fate is still unknown.

Sima Berman is my grandmother on my father's side. My family and I are researching our family roots to understand what happened to her four siblings and her father, who most likely did not survive the Holocaust but still do not appear clearly in the records we have found. We also know that Sima did survive because she made Aliyah to Israel on 28 January 1935 with HaShomer HaTzair. Another major goal is to identify the aunt who survived the Holocaust, later lived in the United States, stayed in contact with Sima for years, and appears to have died around 1980, even though we still do not know her name.

Sima Berman: “My first family was destroyed, so I built a new family!”

What are we trying to find?

  1. What happened to Sima's siblings, Frida, Sonia, Sara, and Chaim, and to her father, Yerucham Meir Buxenbaum? Were they murdered in the Holocaust, and if so, where?
  2. Why do Sima's siblings and father still not appear clearly in the records we have found so far?
  3. Was Chaia really Sima's sister, or was she a close friend? Did she survive the Holocaust?
  4. Who was the aunt who survived the Holocaust, later lived in Los Angeles, and stayed in contact with Sima for years?
  5. Who was the person who sent Sima the letter about her aunt, and could that person help identify the family and what happened to them?

Kovel During the Holocaust

The fate of the Jews of Kovel was tragic. Like many other Jewish communities in Volhynia, the community was destroyed by the Germans, with local collaboration, in a little over a year. This broader history is essential context for understanding what likely happened to Sima's father, siblings, and the rest of her first family.

  • June 1941: the Germans captured Kovel. Murders of local Jews began immediately, including members of the city's intelligentsia.
  • Summer to end of 1941: the Judenrat was established and severe anti-Jewish decrees were imposed, including forced labor, markings, confiscation of property, and public desecration of Torah scrolls. By the end of 1941, about 1,000 Jews had already been murdered.
  • May 1942: two ghettos were established in Kovel, one for those deemed "non-productive" and another for workers and craftsmen.
  • 2–5 June 1942: the first ghetto was liquidated, and around 8,000 Jews were transported to the Bakhiv sand pits and shot.
  • August–October 1942: the second ghetto was liquidated. Many of the remaining Jews were murdered in the new Jewish cemetery and other sites in the area.
  • 1944: Kovel was liberated by the Red Army, but very few Jews remained from a prewar community of roughly 15,000–17,000 people.

Because almost the entire Jewish community of Kovel was destroyed, the absence of clear records for specific relatives does not mean they survived. It often reflects the scale and speed of the destruction.

Key historical sources: Yad Vashem - Kowel community, JewishGen Kovel Yizkor book, Organization of Kovel descendants in Israel, Shtetl Routes - Kovel.

Key Working Problems

  1. Destroyed first family

    We still do not have direct documentary proof for what happened to Sima's four siblings and her father, even though family memory strongly says they did not survive.

  2. Unknown aunt in Los Angeles

    We know there was a surviving aunt who was in touch with Sima in the United States and appears to have died around 1980, but we still do not know her name or exactly how she fits into the family tree.

  3. Surname bridge

    The records still point to more than one surname line: Buxenbaum, Buxenboim, Krakowski, and possibly Nussbaum.
